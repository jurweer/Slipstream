The problem

You're riding together. You're listening alone.

A group ride, and everyone's in their own musical bubble. The fixes that exist were all built for something else:

Spotify Jam / SharePlay

Everyone needs the same paid service, group caps are small, and there's no API to build a real ride experience on.

Silent-disco apps

The streaming tech works, but they're built for parties — and still make everyone tap "join" every single time.

Cardo / Sena intercoms

They solve it — with €300+ of dedicated hardware, and music sharing that's limited or breaks across brands.

The gap Slipstream fills: outdoor group cycling, software-only, any earbuds, a frictionless join, and a shared queue. The defensible part is the ride-day feel — the audio streaming underneath is commodity.
How it works

Prefetch and a shared clock — not live streaming.

Slipstream broadcasts the leader's own owned files. Nobody streams a live signal to your ear; everyone plays their own local copy against a tiny shared clock.

01

The leader pushes ahead of the playhead

Upcoming track files are sent to everyone over WebRTC before they're needed. Followers cache them locally so the music is already on the device when its turn comes.

// files travel early, over the peer connection
02

Everyone plays their own copy to a shared clock

The group syncs to a tiny state — { track, position, playing }. Each rider's phone plays its cached file at that exact position, so it lands in your ears in time.

// { track, position, playing }
03

Signal drops? The music doesn't.

Lose reception in a dead spot and playback keeps going from cache. When you reconnect, you snap straight back to the leader's position. As long as the next tracks are cached, the audio never stops.

// play from cache · snap-to-leader on reconnect
04

Tracks are disposable

Cached tracks evict after they play and get wiped at ride-end. Nothing lingers on anyone's phone — the same throwaway ethos as FileDrop.

// ephemeral cache · wiped when the ride ends
The key requirement

Ride through the dead spots.

Group rides go where signal doesn't. That's the one thing this had to survive — so it's designed around losing the connection, not avoiding it.

When the leader's stream cuts out, your phone keeps playing the track it already has. The moment you're back, it quietly snaps to wherever the leader is now. No silence, no re-buffering, no fumbling with your phone at 30 km/h.

Roadmap

From a working spike to a full paceline.

Now
  • Prove background audio survives a locked / backgrounded screen on real phones — the spike that de-risks the whole PWA bet.
Next
  • Signaling server + pre-register a ride and join a room.
  • WebRTC DataChannel file transfer with a local cache.
  • Shared-clock sync, plus reconnect and snap-to-leader logic.
★ Killer
  • Collaborative queue — any rider drops in their own owned files, and the whole group hears it.
Post-MVP
  • Legal link resolver — paste a Spotify or Apple link, pull the metadata, and match it against owned files in the group or licensed / Creative-Commons catalogs (FMA, Jamendo, ccMixter). Optional per-track Premium sync.
  • Hotspot-share mode with a Wi-Fi-join QR — leader shares data, audio goes phone-to-phone over the LAN.
  • A native shim for true zero-internet hotspot mode.
Deliberate non-goals

The lines we won't cross.

Some things are left out on purpose — not because they're hard, but because they'd make this the wrong kind of product.

✕

No peer-streaming of Spotify or Apple audio

That audio is DRM-locked. It isn't legal or technically possible to relay it peer-to-peer, so we don't try.

✕

No pulling commercial tracks from "free" libraries

That's a piracy pipeline. It's intentionally never built.

✕

The resolver only ever matches clean sources

Owned files, licensed catalogs, or Creative-Commons — nothing else gets played.
